A. P. J. Abdul Kalam

Great Indian scientist and politician who played a leading role in the development of India’s missile and nuclear weapons programs.

About the Legend

A.P.J. Abdul Kalam, known as the "People's President," was born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, India. He served as the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007.

Dr. Kalam was a brilliant scientist and engineer who made significant contributions to India's space and defense programs. He earned a degree in aeronautical engineering from the Madras Institute of Technology and joined the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) in 1958. In 1969, he moved to the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and played a crucial role as the project director of the SLV-III, India's first satellite launch vehicle produced domestically.

One of his most significant achievements was the development of Agni, India's first intermediate-range ballistic missile, which was successfully launched in 1989. Dr. Kalam's dedication and leadership in the field earned him the nickname "Missile Man."

Dr. Kalam's contributions to science and technology were not limited to the defense sector. He also played a key role in India's civilian space program, overseeing the successful launch of India's first satellite, Rohini, in 1980. His leadership and vision helped India make significant strides in space technology.

Apart from his scientific achievements, Dr. Kalam was a prolific writer and speaker. He authored several books, including "Wings of Fire," an autobiography that continues to inspire countless individuals, especially young students. His speeches and lectures on various topics, especially on the importance of education and youth empowerment, left a lasting impact on people across the world.

Dr. Kalam received numerous awards and honors during his lifetime, including the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award, in 1997. He was a true visionary who believed in the power of education and technology to transform India and the world.

A.P.J. Abdul Kalam passed away on July 27, 2015, while doing what he loved—delivering a lecture to students. His legacy continues to inspire generations of young Indians and people worldwide to dream big and work hard to achieve their goals.
